Autonomous Vehicles

What does it do?

Autonomous vehicles are self-piloted robots that use a multitude of sensors to navigate the area around them and often attempt to get to a specific location. They are not just simple implementations such as being able to drive within the lines or take a corner but are reactive to everything around them that a human would be too.

Autonomous vehicles are just beginning to see commercial use in the past couple of years or so and are looking to only get bigger. Although only a few companies are developing vehicles, or more so the sensors and other devices to completely navigate the vehicles through almost all kinds of roads and traffic. Autonomous cars have three primary sensors by default, camera, radar and lidar, along with other things to help steer the car such as GPS and some mechanism to steer the wheel. The camera sensor is self-explanatory, it sees and tries to comprehend the world as a human would, with sight, if it sees a person, it should recognise them and slow down if the system thinks that it may collide with the human. Radar is another of the primary sensors that shootout signals and wait for a return signal, the signal that they send is a microwave signal, unlike ultrasonic sensors or light sensors. The radar sensor is used to accurately gauge objects and their distance around the car. The final primary sensor is lidar, which uses lasers to do a very similar job to radar, it sends out a beam of light, a laser, and waits for a return of that light to accurately gauge objects and their distances, very similar to the radar sensors. All these sensors are sent to the system, along with other complimentary ones like the GPS, to be processed very quickly and direct the car to where it needs to be going.

Autonomy in vehicles is ranked in 6 levels, beginning with level 0 – no automation (self-explanatory), level 1 – hand on/shared control, this could be an automatic gearbox in an automatic car or assisted parking/steering. Level 2 – hands-off, this stage is where the driver does not need to input anything to the car but must stay vigilant as the car may still make a miscalculation and drive incorrectly. Level 3 – eyes off, this is the stage where the driver may let the car do all the work, but if the car begins to ask the driver for assistance, they must be ready to drive the car. Level 4 – mind off, this is the stage at which the driver essentially becomes the passenger, the car can do all the work and the occupant can do as they please in the car. Stage 5 - steering wheel optional, the car is most likely safer than a regular person driving, so it is optional to have a steering wheel for humans to control. On this scale, it is almost personal preference deciding which level is good enough to be called autonomous, on just level 2 the driver does not need to have any input, just to be ready if it is needed, and level 5 is of course fully autonomous as it requires and recommends no input. All these levels are available in the vehicle market from levels 0 – 5, being a manual car, automatic car or similar, vehicles like a Tesla, and for the top three a range of different commercial vehicles being offered for hire, like a taxi. This technology will only continue to expand its market, and I am sure that people can expect more developed and higher-level autonomy vehicles driving around in the next decade or so.

Should this be the case and autonomous vehicles reach this level, collisions would go down along with travel time and most likely the worry of people having to drive. Cybersecurity

What does it do and how does it work?

Cybersecurity aims to protect systems, networks, and programs from digital attack through the implementation of effective cybersecurity measures. Digital attacks can consist of the viewing, altering and or destruction of sensitive information. These attacks can interrupt businesses processes and extort money from users (Cisco, 2022).

There are multiple types of cybersecurity threats that must be addressed. Phishing is the sending of fake emails that aim to trick the recipient into thinking that it is from a trusted source and thus leads to misplaced trust. This misplaced trust is then abused to steal sensitive information such as credit card numbers and login information. Ransomware infiltrates a computer system through phishing emails with malicious attachments or downloading infected files from a malicious website. It then extorts the user by preventing access to files on the computer system until a ransom is paid and even after paying such a ransom the files may or may not be returned (Cisco, 2022). Malware is software that aim to destroy or damage a computer or computer system (Cisco, 2022). Social engineering aims to deceive the user to get them to provide sensitive information. They may pretend to from a reputable company or make threats to get the user to provide information such as login details or for them to provide a monetary payment.

To protect from such attacks, multiple layers of protection must be spread among the computers, networks and, programs or data that the individual or organization seeks to keep safe (Cisco, 2022). On the user side protection consists of the basic data security principles such as choosing strong passwords, not opening unknown email attachments, and backing up their data (Cisco, 2022). In terms of technology, it is important to protect endpoint devices such as computers, smart devices along with routers, networks, and the cloud (Cisco, 2022). The most common technology for their protection includes firewalls, DNS (Domain Name System) filtering, anti-malware, anti-virus, and email checking solutions. Firewalls monitor outgoing and incoming traffic and allows or prevents the transfer data packets based on a set of security rules (Force Point, 2021). DNS filtering blocks access to certain sites of a particular category or type that are considered a threat. The DNS filter then blocks the IP address of these sites. These helps limit the amount of a threats a network is exposed to (Webroot, 2022). Malware helps to protect systems from malicious software. To do this it uses signature-based detection, behaviour-based detection, and sandboxing. Signature-based uses a set of established software components and digital signature commonly associated with malware. Behaviour-based looks at how the software acts within a system, if it is suspicious, it will be detected by the anti-malware. Sandboxing runs a suspicious file outside the system such as in a virtual environment with restricted access therefore if the file tries to access other files it will be blocked (Rosencrance, L., 2021). Anti-virus is also like anti-malware in that it checks computer programs and files against known malware. It uses specific detection for known malware, generic detection for known parts of malware and heuristic detection for suspicious file structures (Verizon Fios, 2022).

What is the state of art of this new technology?

Due to the emergence of pro-active threat detection and prevention, heuristic-based and behaviour-based detection and prevention systems are currently considered the most advanced form of threat detection. NGAVs or “next generation antiviruses” implement such measures to counter threats unlike older AVs which mainly use signature-based detection which can only respond to recognized threats. This technology can not only block malware, but also malware-less attacks despite any tactics, techniques and procedures attackers decide to pull (Crowdstrike, 2021).

What can be done now?

The state-of-the-art antivirus technology (NGAV) uses artificial intelligence along with behavioural detection, machine learning algorithms and exploit mitigation. This helps ensure that known and unknown threats can be predicted and thus prevented (Crowdstrike, 2021). Using machine learning algorithms NGAVs can perform signature-less malware protection which can determine whether a file might be malicious through recognizing typical malicious code and behaviour (Crowdstrike, 2021). Exploit mitigation helps to detect and prevent any program code that takes advantage of any vulnerabilities on the computer or computer system to gain unauthorised access or to perform malicious activities. Currently NGAVs are cloud-native which allow them to be fully operational in seconds with little to no setup (Crowdstrike, 2021).

What does it do and how does it work?

Machine learning is a subset of Artificial intelligence that focus on mass data processing to identify or predict the future trend. (IBM Cloud Education, 2020) By parsing massive dataset, machine can learn, identify data for automation, these included but not limit to: music identification and content recommendation. (Grieve, 2020) Machine learning can be supervised, which human telling machine the knowledge (for example image identification) (Russell & Norvig, 2020, p. 653) or finding the pattern on its own with unsupervised learning. (Grieve, 2020)

Neural network, akin to neuron cells in our brain, are clusters of "nodes" interconnect to each other, each node has a threshold for data characteristic with "weight" to redirect to the next layer, this process will repeat itself until it reaches the output layer, the machine then decide what the input is based on the weight of each possible outcome. (Nicholson, 2020) The basic algorithm used by neural network is Q-learning with update equation

Q(s,a) ← Q(s,a) + α[R(s,a,s') + γ max Q(s',a') − Q(s,a)]. Q(s,a) ← Q(s,a) + 𝛼[R(s,a,s′) + 𝛾 max Q(s′,a′) − Q(s,a)].

The basic principle is we first randomize the weight of all nodes, for each state (s) with all possible actions (a) we take the maximum value, and we adjust the weight for each action (nodes) accordingly, the amount of adjustment is determined by learning rate α.

Natural Language Processing

What does it do?

Natural language processing, or NLP, is a branch of artificial intelligence that focuses on enabling computers to understand human language, including nuances and intent within spoken or written text. Due to the irregularities contained within human language, such as homophones, idioms, metaphors, exceptions to grammar rules and sentence structure variations, it is quite a challenge to create programs that can accurately understand the intended meaning of text or voice data. To allow computers to make sense of all this, there are several NLP tasks that are performed to break down the data and make sense of it. These tasks include speech recognition, grammatical tagging, word sense disambiguation, named entity recognition, co-reference resolution, and sentiment analysis. Speech recognition is the task that converts spoken word into written text. This can be a challenging task due to the differences in how people speak, having to account for accents, talking quickly or with slurring, and incorrect grammar to name a few elements. Grammatical tagging is the way in which NLP determines the meaning of a part of speech based on its context and use. For example, the difference between bow as a noun and bow as a verb. Word sense disambiguation is how the meaning of a word is selected by analyzing its semantics and determining what meaning makes the most sense in that context. This is how NLP deals with homonyms and similar situations. Named entity recognition is simply used to identify words as important entities, such as places or names. Similarly, co-reference resolution is how NLP determines when two words refer to the same entity, commonly this occurs with matching pronouns to entities but also with metaphors and idioms. Finally, sentiment analysis is when subjective qualities are extracted from the text, such as attitudes and emotions. These tasks make up the subsection of natural language processing called natural language understanding, which as the name implies, is concerned with the understanding of human language. The other subsection of NLP is natural language generation, which focuses on giving the computer the tools to generate its own text mimicking human language. Natural language generation used to simply use templates and fill them out with data but has since improved to be much more dynamic with the introduction of neural networks.

Initially, the first natural language processors were coded by hand and used a set of rules to perform limited tasks, but this approach was limited to smaller scales and struggled to keep up with ever increasing amounts of text or voice data. Since then, NLP has come to use a combination of algorithms and deep learning models to automatically analyze each element of given data and assign a statistical likelihood to the various possible meanings. Additionally, the use of deep learning models and techniques based on both convolutional and recurrent neural networks allows NLP to learn as it works to process and understand this data. As the AI behind NLP improves, it is thought that eventually NLP will become more proactive in its uses. As an example, if a user extensively uses a virtual agent to order food, the agent could begin to prompt the user when it thinks the ser will want to eat, or even recommend alternatives to the user. Similarly, in a business environment an NLP could analyze the data from employee emails and calls and make suggestions for email structures or alternative communication methods for the business that would be more cost efficient. This proactive NLP is beginning to be explored as neural network and AI technology improves, and the potential uses in both a personal and business environment are numerous.

There are several fields that natural language processing is already impacting, such as healthcare, education, finance, data science and business. This is due to the ability of NLP to process a large amount of data quickly and accurately. For example, in the healthcare industry NLP can be used to draw conclusions from patient data about symptom patterns and patient behavior. Additionally, the elements of natural language understanding can be used to assist in research with mental health and understand the emotions and feelings of patients. Another relevant example would be in education, where NLP is used to detect plagiarism and grammar quickly. Not just assisting teachers, students can also make use of NLP, for assistance with writing or to better research with data mining and summarization. These are just some of the fields that NLP is already making an impact. These uses will only improve with time as the technology behind natural language processing gets better.
